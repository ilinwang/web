Statement of Research Interests

I-Lin Wang

ilin@isye.gatech.edu

        I am interested in the fields of applied Operations Research and design & analysis of algorithms. I have an undergraduate major in Aerospace Engineering, and since 1994 I have studied Operations Research at several of the best institutes in the U.S. I have learned linear programming, nonlinear programming, network optimization, and algorithms from the OR center at MIT; stochastic processes and simulation from MSE, Stanford University; and integer programming, combinatorial optimization and graph theory from ISyE, Georgia Tech.  In other words, I am well trained by the best schools in their strongest fields.  Also, I am confident in my ability to face new challenges in new environments.

        Network optimization and algorithm design are the fields that I have devoted most of my research efforts to. My Master's thesis, "Implementing the Premultiplier Method for Minimum Cost Flow Problem", was the first implementation of a new polynomial time network simplex algorithm proposed by Dr. James B. Orlin of MIT. Subsequently, the communication network system research lab of Fujitsu Research Lab in Japan gave me a position as a foreign researcher to work on their telecommunication project. I proposed and implemented several wavelength assignment and routing algorithms (later known as FINDEM, the Fujitsu Integrated Network Design Method) for their WDM management prototype. At Georgia Tech, my Ph.D. dissertation, "Shortest Paths and Multicommodity Network Flows", gives several new multiple pairs shortest path algorithms and proposes new multicommodity network flow algorithms. My new shortest path algorithms are the first in five decades designed specifically for finding shortest paths for multiple origin-destination (OD) pairs. Overall, these experiences show my potential as a strong researcher who is able to independently propose new ideas, even for old subjects.

        With my strong OR background and solid programming skills, there is a wide range of theoretical and applied research that I can investigate. In particular, I am interested in the following topics:


  • Supply Chain Logistics:

    •        Many logistics problems are network optimization problems. For example, routing problems for products, vehicles, or personnel; traffic assignment and equilibrium problems; product scheduling and planning problems; and freight network design problems are usually variants of multicommodity network flow problems.

           I have worked in the Integrated Logistics Support (ILS) group of Aero-Industry- Development-Center (AIDC) in 1991~1993 for two years on logistics problems relating to aircraft parts. Since 2000, I have been involved in the Air Cargo project of The Logistics Institute (TLI) in my department and TLI-Asia-Pacific (TLI-AP) at the National University of Singapore. Having studied the cargo terminal of Changi airport, I am familiar with the logistics problems that such terminals face. Currently TLI-AP has several joint research projects with my advisor, Dr. Ellis Johnson, a pioneering researcher in the fields of airline operations research such as crew scheduling, fleet assignment, and revenue management. I am, and will continue to be, involved in their research of Air Cargo network problems as proposed in our TLI-AP white paper, ¡§The Asia Pacific Air Cargo Systemби.

           My connection to TLI-AP and TLI may be helpful in future collaborations to solve logistics problems in Taiwan.

  • Telecommunication Networks:

    •        Message routing between many OD pairs, packet routing on virtual circuit data networks, routing on ring networks, and many telecommunication network design problems can be formulated as multicommodity network flow problems, which is a major topic of my Ph.D. thesis.

           With my previous experience and connection to the people in Fujitsu Research Lab., possible joint research opportunities may be identified in internet related fields.

  • Bioengineering and Bioinformatics:

    •        Bio-related research is currently a hot topic. Many biological questions are, in fact, optimization problems and can be solved with success by operations research techniques such as integer programming and combinatorial optimization algorithms. For example, gene expression array analysis, sequence comparisons, gene finding and prediction, and structure prediction all can be modeled as optimization problems.

           In addition, some biomedical problems such as cancer treatment planning and radio surgery for tumors can be formulated as mixed integer programming problems and solved by OR techniques.

           My department, ISyE at Georgia Tech, has several researchers working on these problems. To the best of my knowledge, I have not seen much work done in these fields in Taiwan. With my OR training and connection to the faculty of ISyE, I am able to investigate this new research right away.

  • Research in Optimization Techniques:

    •        As suggested in the last chapter of my Ph.D. thesis, there is potential for innovative research in shortest path algorithms and multicommodity network flow algorithms. Research regarding new methods such as the primal-dual least squares method for solving linear programming problems is also a promising research topic to investigate. Since I have been working on these problems for a few years, I am familiar with many state-of-the-art optimization techniques and am ready for further research on these topics.

           By proposing research projects that involve not only myself but also colleagues and students in this department, I hope that my knowledge, capability, personality and connection to other people and institutes in Singapore, Japan, and USA can make a positive contribution to the students and faculty of this department and this school.
